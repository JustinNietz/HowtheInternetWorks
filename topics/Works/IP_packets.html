IP Addresses, Packets and Routing

What is an IP Address?

An IP Address is a single computer’s address on the internet. This address is just a number, similar to a phone number, that is unique to each computer or device at the edge of the network. It forms part of one of the most important protocols used in internet communication called the internet protocol (IP).

An IP address is a bunch of numbers organized in a hierarchy. Each of the numbers are represented in bits. IP Addresses are 32 bits long with 8 bits for each part of the address. This version, called IPv4, provides more than 4 billion unique addresses for devices connected to the internet.

Four billion, however, won’t be enough in the near future! We are currently in a multi-year transition to a longer IP address format called IPv6 which uses 128 bits per address, provides 340 undecillion unique addresses.

What is a Packet?

If you have a very large image that you want to send over the internet, that image is made up of tens of millions of bits or ones and zeroes, too many to send in one packet. The computer sending the image can quickly break it up into hundreds or thousands of smaller parts called packets.

A Packet – made up of binary – is a sequence of ones and zeroes sent physically through wired and wireless networks. Packets do not choose their routes through the distributed network. Each packet only has the internet address of where it came from and where it's going.

What is a Router?

Special computers on the internet called Routers act like traffic managers to keep packets moving smoothly. If one route is congested, individual packets may travel different routes through the internet, and they arrived at the destination at slightly different times or even out of order.

As part of the internet protocol, every router keeps track of multiple paths for sending packets. It chooses the cheapest available path for each piece of data based on destination IP address for the packet. Cheapest refers to time and non-technical factors such as politics and relationships between companies.

Often the best route for data to travel is not the most direct. Having options for paths makes the network fault tolerant, which means the networks can keep sending packets even if something goes horribly wrong.

Step-by-Step: How an Internet Message is sent

  1. Data being sent is divided into packets (Max size = 64K, 65535 bytes)
  2. Packet has a header with some technical information and a FROM address, TO address and the actual data. Each header is 60 bytes
  3. Packets divided in to multiple parts contain the order of the packet
  4. Packets move on the Internet along different paths each taking different times
  5. Packets are reordered
  6. Received packets are acknowledged back to the sender
  7. Sender resends packets that do not acknowledge

Why do we use IP Addresses, Packets, and Routing?

The key principle of the internet is reliability. The internet is ultimately a set of protocols. A Protocol is a well-known set of rules and standards used to communicate between machines.

The transmission control protocol (TCP) manages the sending and receiving of all your data as packets. Think of it like a guaranteed mail service. For example, when you request a song on your device from Spotify, it sends the song broken up into many packets. When your packets arrive, TCP does a full inventory and sends back acknowledgements of each packet received. If all packets are there, TCP signs for delivery and you are done. If it finds some packets are missing, it will not sign the acknowledgement. For each missing or incomplete packet, Spotify will resend them.

The TCP and Router systems is they are scalable. They can work with 8 devices or 8 billion devices. Because of these principles of fault tolerant and redundancy, the more routers we add the more reliable the internet becomes.
