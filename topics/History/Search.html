The Search Engine

We forget that a little more than 15 years ago the web just looked like page after page of plain text and long lists of underline sentences.

Creation of Yahoo

When Jerry Yang and David Filo’s teacher went on a yearlong sabbatical, they started goofing around on the web. They were trying to find a way of using the Internet to win a Stanford fantasy basketball league. In hunting around for obscure sports data on the Internet, Jerry and David were taking the first steps on the road to search.

They decided to create a simple directory that could show web users how to find stuff online. It was just a bunch of categories and sub-categories that you could look around in. Millions of users from around the globe flocked to their site, and they soon realized they needed a shorter, snappier name for their site; Yahoo was born.

A venture capitalist named Michael Moritz decided to pay Yahoo’s young founders a visit in their Stanford trailer. No one had figured out how to make money off the Internet, but Yahoo would change all that. It helped launched the gold rush that would soon engulf the web.

Advertising Online

At the dawn of the web, no one used it for business or commerce. The arguments around the impending commercialization of the web were fierce, and they boiled down to just one word: advertising.

Late in 1995, Yahoo began taking banner ads which proved those arguments didn’t matter as the user base kept on multiplying. For the first time, Yahoo had shown it was possible to make money on the web. It was a crucial moment and meant one thing: the web boom had begun. In 1996, Yahoo faced an assortment of challengers, but their most formidable rival was a company called Excite. Rather than a list of sites complied and sorted into categories by human beings, Excite’s software would crawl the web, find pages that contain the term you’d enter.

The World of Portals

By 1997, companies like Yahoo and Excite were busy turning themselves into what were known as Portals - one-stop destinations jam-packed with diversions from stock tickers to email to chat room. They were designed to turn users into a captive audience for the benefit of advertisers. By focusing on the flash, they lost sight of what it was that brought users to them in the first place: the need to find things on the expanding web.

The Creation of Google

Google’s founders were Larry Page and Sergey Brin from Stanford University. Larry’s insight started with the notion that the number of times a given web page was linked to by other sites might be a measure of how useful or how relevant it was. As they put it in a now famous academic paper in 1998, “In essence, Google interprets a link from page A to page B as a vote by page A for page B. Google assesses of pages importance by the votes it receives.”

Larry’s insight about link counting was simple but kind of brilliant. It was the core of Google’s incredible success. When Google was launched onto the Stanford website, there was so much traffic to Google that it nearly brought the entire Stanford University’s internet down. The university administration said they must move off the campus.

Funding for Google

Vinod Khosla, a venture capitalist invested in Excite, believed that if Excite bought Google, it would give them an edge over Yahoo. The CEO of Excite didn’t care, however, as he believed Excite’s search engine was good. At the time they did not know Excite turned down one of the most profitable deals in history by not acquiring Google. Larry and Sergey sought out a professor from Stanford was also a serial entrepreneur, David Cheriton. David then called up his friend, Andy Bechtolsheim, who was a co-founder of Sun Microsystems Inc. Andy was so impressed, he wrote them a check for 100,000 dollars on the spot. They received checks from other Angel investors totaling 1 million dollars.

Google wanted to organize all the information in the world making it searchable and universally accessible. However, they were losing money because they did not put banner ads on their site. They simple could not stomach the idea of Google turning into just another portal.

Google’s Advertisement Dilemma

They wanted to make sure they left people with a great experience but also with advertisements that made sense to people. They wanted ads in a more user friendly, webified form. They could not think of what that would be.

In true Silicon Valley style, they found their solution by copying it from Idealab. It was Bill Gross who ran Idealab and cracked the riddle of internet advertising. Bill Gross saw before anyone else that a search engine could be and probably would be the world’s most powerful and efficient market research tool.

Gross realized that keywords, which are terms you type into a search engine, could be worth a fortune. In 1998 he launched a site based on keywords and sponsored links that would eventually be known as Overture. It became an instant success.

Back in Silicon Valley, Google would see his idea as the solution to its business model quandary. The pair arranged to meet with Bill Gross. They talked about ways to blend the great monetization that Overture had and the incredibly relevant search results that Google had. Google walked away from the talks without cutting any kind of deal and then, shortly after, they released a service called Adwords that was uncannily similar to Overtures. Google would tweak Gross’ original idea in important ways. It would separate the ads from the so-called organic search results.

Google's Public Release

What we now understand as the internet economy was being born with Google as its most essential player. August 19, 2004 Google went public, listing its shares for sale on the NASDAQ stock market. Google IPO caused a frenzy of speculation and anticipation especially among those who hoped it might revive a tech sector still recovering from the dot-com crash. Google became the fastest growing company ever.

Google's Future

With Google personalizing ads, there became a fear of privacy. Terry Winograd, a former professor of Com Sci at Stanford had these parting words: “People are going to feel there really is a danger in having too much information stored about everything they’ve done, everything they’ve searched for and were going to see more and more about that.”
