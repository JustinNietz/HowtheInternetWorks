Communication Uprising

New online companies arose in the late 1990s and the early 2000s; their purpose was to create a democratic medium that was controlled by no one but shaped by everyone who used it. The Internet had an enormous impact on our daily life, but now it was time for internet users to change the Web itself for their benefit. This revolution was called Web 2.0 .

Napster, the Pioneer

These goal of this new businesses was to give power to users to control their digital content consumption. The greatest pioneer of them was Napster, which opened the door for inventing the idea of digital goods . Napster was founded in 1999. It provided a software application which is served as a platform for sharing digital audio files among users. This meant if you had an album by a musician, you could share it with others who are interested in getting it. At the same time, you had the opportunity to look at other users’ lists of digital audio files and download whatever you liked. This whole process was possible thanks to inventing the method of compressing audio files without any quality damage ( MP3 ); it resulted in much smaller file size, so then uploading and downloading a song became much faster. It was a revolutionary idea and became immensely popular among young adults.

There was only one problem; the copyright owners of these songs (producers, studios, and musicians) did not get any financial compensation from this file-sharing mechanism. Napster got sued (main suers: Dr. Dre and Metallica) because of its illegal activity. The lawsuit attracted attention, which resulted in more and more users joined Napster. Even so, in 2001 Napster had to shut down due to the verdict of the lawsuit. However, after this legal battle new companies emerged to fight for digital rights.

The Revolutionary Companies

Although Napster lost its battle for digital rights, it made a vast impact on the media industry. New companies emerged from Napster's ashes to fight for people’s rights in this new digital era. Let us have a look at two of them.

Digg

Digg.com’s main goal was to eliminate the old method when executives and editors decided which news is available for you. Digg.com created a system wherein your peers can send in and vote on news articles; this resulted in which article is worth publishing on the website. Briefly, the users can decide what is important news for them.

YouTube

YouTube was created in 2005 because its founders thought it is hard to distribute and produce your digital content within traditional media. They wanted to provide a platform where your content can be easily accessible to users, and you do not lose your rights over your product after publishing it. Their motto summarizes this efficiently: Broadcast yourself! However, YouTube had also legal battles like Napster because users did not upload only user-generated content but copyrighted videos too. YouTube luckily survived the lawsuits thanks to the resources of Google, which owns YouTube. The website fights against uploading copyrighted content, but it is still a problem.

The Social Network Was Born

The Web gave us another way to communicate with those whom we already knew and expanded the numbers of whom we could meet. The first site which is worth mentioning in this arena is Myspace.

Myspace

Myspace – founded in 2003 – was one of the first sites which we can call a social media site. Every user could have a profile site, which mimicked the user’s taste in décor (band posters, friends’ pictures in the background). You had the opportunity to listen to music with the help of a built-in music player during spending time on the website. It became so popular that musicians and Hollywood studios started to use this site for promotion. In a short time, competition arrived; Facebook was founded in 2004.

Facebook

Facebook started as a small project in a dorm room at Harvard, but it became widely successful; the number of its users doubles every 6 months worldwide. The basic idea behind Facebook is that Mark Zuckerberg, the founder, thinks the most trusted people in our life are our friends, colleagues, and family, and mapping out these relationships is a valuable item. These relationships give us billions of connections that can be reorganized into so-called social graphs . With these social graphs, we can target people with advertisements efficiently.

Internet Renaissance

After the burst of the dot.com bubble, a new era came where the crowd got the power to contribute to the human culture. The possibilities are vast: anyone can be an amateur journalist as a blogger; you can make your advertisement on Craigslist; or you can help to build an enormous digital encyclopedia on Wikipedia. Finally, Sir Tim Berners-Lee, the founder of the World Wide Web, is satisfied because the Web took that path he wanted in the first place: Let people create and not just consume products. The Internet needs to be a two-way road.
