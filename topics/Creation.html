The Invention of the Internet

We live in the age of the Internet – a global network of computer-based communication. But where did the Internet come from? How did it get started, and who built this essential and playful tool to millions, and where is it going?

Visionary Behind the Internet

The seminal idea behind the Internet was envisioned by a psychologist name J.C.R. Licklider, a professor at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in the 1950s. His vision was that computers would allow you to communicate better, furthermore, this would let you connect all the computers together, so that they might be more useful in the sharing of information among people.

The Reason We Have the Internet Today

The Cold War became the catalyst for the expansion of computers, which were as big as a room then. The launch of Sputnik by the Soviet Union in October 1957 caused President Eisenhower to create the bureau at the defense department named ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency) to compete with the Russians. At the time, ARPA was in charge of the space program, which also included computer science. Computer communication was in the embryonic stage but was making progress.

Queuing Theory

In 1960 at MIT, a student named Leonard Kleinrock began applying Queuing Theory to data transmission. Like letters going through post offices, Kleinrock showed how packages of data would queue or line up at nodes of a communications network as they transited through. This way he could determine the overall speed of the network. He then showed how to speed up the network by implementing two techniques:

  • Demand access – You don’t get it until you need it is the key idea for effective resource sharing with data network.
  • Distributed control – No one’s switch, device, or authority should control the entire network. Rather, every switch should share in that control. It should decide the routing procedure to decide how much traffic is allowed in where it is going, when to do all the functions in distributed fashion.

Telephones and Packets

The telephone system became another catalyst for the creation of the Internet. If the connection path between a telephone call is broken, the connection is cut off. This weakness became a matter of national security in the early 1960s as they feared a Soviet attack which would cripple their communications in effective retaliation. Dr. Paul Baran, who was hired to fix this problem, helped to create Hot Potato Routing, which meant a message transiting a network got to where it was going even if part of the network was destroyed.

This routing led to Packet Switching Packet Switching. The network chops it up a document into a lot of pieces (packets). Each packet was like a postcard with a to and from address that would get launched into the network and operate independently. The final destination takes all these packets and puts them back into a long document at the end. This design for a network, a Packet Switching Network, gets its message through better than telephones as long as some part of the network functions.

Bob Taylor’s Frustrations

The first computer networks stem from the frustrations of a single ARPA scientist, Bob Taylor, who needed a separate terminal to log into each of several time-shared computers at research centers across the country. He knew a single terminal would suffice if all the computers were in a common network.

Taylor was given a million dollars to work on this problem where he recruited Larry Roberts, who built the world’s first experimental connection between two computers. Roberts was inspired by a fellow engineer, who suggested building identical special-purpose mini computers, called Interface Messaging Processors ( IMPs ), to form the network and tie each computer to one of them.

He sought out the engineers to build the Advance Research Projects Agency’s computer network, the ARPANET. The plan was to network 4 identical IMPs. Each one would be attached to a different kind of mainframe computer at three different universities across California and one in Utah.

BBN and IMPS and Grad Students

A small Cambridge Massachusetts company known as BBN won the contract and went to work building the IMPs for the ARPANET on January 1st, 1969. Both BBN and UCLA were under a tight deadline. Writing the code to have the computers communicate with the IMPs were left to the graduate students or staff members. There was concern with the phone lines, which were built for voice traffic and not data or running the mainframe computers nonstop. By the end of 1969, all four sites were interconnected and had worked the way it was intended.

ARPANET

In April 1971, the ARPANET had 18 mainframe computers hooked into the network. At first there wasn’t much to do beyond transferring data files among its members but then a new capability was added that would take the usefulness of computer networking to a new level. Raymond Tomlinson created this killer application called email. Tomlinson gave the world the @ sign.

Internet begun in 1969 as an experimental computer network. Anyone could contribute ideas about its use and how to improve it. Ideas were offered as requests for comments so everyone would free feel to discuss and criticize them. By 1972, the ARPANET had fewer than 25 sites online, yet more were coming. What spakred the increase was the first international conference on computers and communication sponsored by ARPA.

The ARPANET network map in 1974.

Networks

Soon other networks emerged. LAN (local area networks) linked computers throughout offices; WAN (wide area networks) linked computers across buildings or campuses. Each new network was like a foreign country speaking its own language and independent of one another. They needed a common protocol for them to communicate.

In 1973, Ben Cerf and Bob Kahn teamed up to develop a set of rules or procedures known as the TCP/IP protocol that, if followed by different computer networks would allow them to pass their message packets back and forth. The gateway, as Cerf and Kahn named it, knew how to talk to each of the networks it was connected to. In 1983, TCP/IP was adopted as a standard.

Under 1980s technology it was difficult to research a topic. Tim Berners-Lee, a physicist at the CERN Research Institute in Switzerland developed the software to follow threads of knowledge more easily within the information stored throughout the internet. By using a computer mouse to click on a word or phrase linked across the Internet, one could instantaneously follow a link from site to site around the web in pursuit of a topic. The crisscrossing of these threads across the globe reminded him of a web so he named his invention the World Wide Web.

On June 9, 1992, Congress passed a bill taking the Internet out of the exclusive hands of government and into the public. The Internet had crossed the Rubicon.
