The Creation of the World Wide Web

The internet age began with a vicious power struggle to decide who would control the future of the just emerging web, officially remembered as the Browser War.

Back then the web was very different. There were hardly any web pages and they consisted of nothing but line after line of boring text. There was hardly anything worth finding and no way to find it anyway. You had to be a big computer fanatic to find your way around the Internet.

The Creation of Mosaic

At the University of Illinois Computer Science, a student named Mark Andreessen had an idea. He said that “The internet was only really for academics, researchers and scientists, and there wasn’t anything that ordinary people could do with it. But it was so useful to the people who were using it that I figured everyone should be able to use it.”

With the help of friends, they worked to make the web more intuitive to use by adding images, pictures, audio, and video capabilities. Their efforts created the world’s first graphical web browser, and in the fall of 1993 they posted it, called Mosaic , online and made it free. For Mosaic to go truly public, however, it needed a giant influx of cash.

The Company Named Netscape

Jim Clark, a high school dropout who later in life became a Computer Science Professor at Stanford, contacted Andreessen. He was considering starting a new software company and wanted Andreessen on board. They plotted how to turn Mosaic into the basis of a business venture. They named their company Netscape and even hired undergraduate students from the University of Illinois, where Andreessen was a student.

The web browser would change the world, but although Jim Clark may have grasped that insight, most people didn’t, including Bill Gates.

The Microsoft Giant

Bill Gates created the most important and profitable company of them all. In two decades, Microsoft had established a near monopoly over PC operating systems, and it used that monopoly to gain a vice-like grip over the entire computer industry. His online system was called the Microsoft Network.

Bill Gates’ story began in 1975 when at the age of 19 he dropped out of Harvard to start Microsoft with his friend Paul Allen. Gates’ vision was simple and ambitious: a PC on every desktop in every home and every office, and all running Microsoft software. By 1993 his dream was close to becoming a reality. About 90% of the world’s PCs were running Microsoft Windows. They played very rough with their competitors who created their own operating systems. They defeated companies such as Lotus, WordPerfect, and even IBM were defeated.

Netscape vs. Microsoft

Netscape culturally already perceived Microsoft as the company to take out when all other companies at the time were afraid to face them. On October 13, 1994 after months of coding, Netscape’s new browser, Navigator, was released, and it was an instant success.

In the computer business, the companies that have the most power control what’s known as a Software Platform ,software platform (the software which other programs run on top). With this near monopoly over the Windows operating system, Microsoft controlled the ultimate platform.

With the release of Navigator, however, many in Silicon Valley began to imagine a different future where the browser could be an alternative platform.

Microsoft's Biggest Threat

The problem for Netscape was that Bill Gates now perceived the company to be the primary threat to their continued dominance.

Gates dispatched a special group of envoys to Netscape’s headquarters. Each side had a separate take on the meeting:

  • Microsoft version: Had this free roaming discussion with them about how we would work with Netscape, how would we work with them on web servers and browsers.
  • Netscape version: Microsoft offered a deal to Netscape they couldn’t refuse; one million dollars were offered for unlimited access to all their technology. If they didn’t take it, Microsoft was just going to copy everything they did.

Microsoft's Revenge

Microsoft planned to drive Netscape to an early grave with a web browser of its own: Internet Explorer . Microsoft had advantages in building their browser, including vast finances and reserves of coding talent. Gates also used his army of salesman to stop PC manufacturers from installing anything other than Internet explorer by using dirty methods.

Netscape had to charge money for its browser, but Microsoft was able to give Internet Explorer away for free. They were able to bundle it into the Windows OS. When Internet Explorer 4 (IE4), came out, the Browser War was over. By September 1997, Microsoft had won. From that point on, it was all downhill for Netscape. A year later, the company was acquired by a bigger company called America Online (AOL).

Microsoft in Court

In 1998, Rebeck launched an Antitrust lawsuit against Microsoft on behalf of his clients, which included Netscape. He accused them of using their monopoly to engage in a wide variety of predatory practices to prevent consumers from accessing Netscape’s products.

In court, Gates lied about multiple topics, one of which included the meeting with Netscape. He stated he had no knowledge of that meeting but then evidence came out of an email he sent his high-level staff members about it. The federal judge over the case issued his verdict: guilty and recommended the company be broken up causing its stock market value to drop by thirty billion dollars overnight.

Eventually, a federal appeals court, though it agreed with the judgement that Microsoft broke the law, decided the verdict was too harsh and rescinded the order to split the company.

Gates during the deposition

Conclusion

Although Microsoft won the war, the revolution sparked by Netscape unleashed a new generation of startups companies like Google that have made Microsoft look old, clueless and lumbering just as Microsoft did to IBM two decades ago.
